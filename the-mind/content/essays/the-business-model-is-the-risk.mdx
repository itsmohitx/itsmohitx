Most arguments about AI risk are arguments about the machine. Will it be too capable? Will it deceive us? Will it want something we don't? These are real questions. They are also, I think, a distraction from the risk that is already here, already scaling, and already paying for itself — which is not the machine at all. It's the business model wrapped around it.

We have run this experiment once already, at lower resolution, and we called it the attention economy.

## The economy is co-produced

The lazy version of the attention-economy critique blames the platforms: a few companies built engagement-maximizing machines and pointed them at our minds. That's half of it. The other half is us — or rather, the enormous bottom-up industry of people chasing the same incentive. :sidenote[Watch what happens to any new platform the moment a creator-payout program launches. The behavior of millions reorganizes around the metric within weeks. That's not a top-down conspiracy; it's a price signal.] The clipping farms, the rage-bait accounts, the engagement consultants, the thumbnail-optimization cottage industry. The attention economy isn't something done *to* a passive audience by villains above. It's co-produced: platforms optimize from the top, and a vast market of money-chasers optimizes from below, and the two meet in the middle, in your feed.

That structure matters because it tells you the problem is not a particular company you could regulate away. The problem is an objective function that millions of independent actors are all climbing at once. The platform is just the largest, best-funded climber.

## Now make the climber smarter

Here is the move that should worry you. Take that objective — maximize engagement, maximize time, maximize the probability of the next click — and hand it to a system far more capable than a recommendation algorithm. Don't change the goal. Just upgrade the optimizer.

You do not get a wise assistant. You get a vastly better behavior-capture engine. A system that can model you individually, predict what will move you, generate the exact stimulus in real time, and adapt faster than you can notice it adapting. The intelligence everyone is worried about doesn't become dangerous because it develops alien goals. It becomes dangerous because it is extraordinarily good at the goals we already gave it — and the goal we gave the last generation of this technology was *capture*.

This is why I think "is the model aligned?" is the wrong first question. A perfectly obedient model, pointed at engagement, is the catastrophe. It will do precisely what it was asked, and what it was asked is to find the most effective way to occupy a human being.

## The risk lives in the incentive

If the risk is the business model, then the interventions that matter are the ones that change the incentive, not the ones that tune the algorithm. What does the system get *paid* for? That single question predicts more about how an AI product will behave than any amount of model-card documentation.

A system paid for your attention will, in the limit, learn to take it from you. A system paid for outcomes you'd endorse on reflection — did you learn the thing, did you finish the task, did you feel good about the hour afterward — bends in a completely different direction. Same underlying model. Opposite consequences. The difference is entirely in the economics.

This connects to a larger pattern. The attention economy is the :trail[recapture pattern]{slug=the-recapture-pattern} running on AI: a liberating technology — near-free access to intelligence — being pulled back into concentration through the oldest available business model. And it's one of the quiet engines behind :trail[the two-tier future]{slug=a-scene-from-the-two-tier-future}, because the people who own the optimizer and the people who are optimized are not on the same side of the screen.

The optimistic reading — and I am, stubbornly, an optimist — is that this is fixable, and fixable without slowing the technology down at all. We don't need the models to be less capable. We need to pay them for something better than our attention. The machine was never the problem. The price was.
