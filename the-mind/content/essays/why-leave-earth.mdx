The case for becoming multiplanetary is usually made badly, which is a shame, because the case is strong. It gets made as either escapism — abandon the wrecked Earth for a shiny new one — or as a rich man's vanity project. Both framings are wrong, and both make the idea easy to dismiss. Let me try to make it properly.

## The defensive argument

Start with the unglamorous version. Every human being who has ever lived has lived in one place. That is an extraordinary concentration of risk, and we would not accept it in any other domain. We don't keep all the backups in one building. We don't fly the whole leadership team on one plane. And yet the entire human story — every language, every song, every person you love, the whole accumulated inheritance of consciousness on this planet — sits on a single rock, exposed to a single set of failure modes.

Most of those failure modes are low-probability. But the cost, if any of them lands, is *everything* — not a setback, the end of the story. :sidenote[The expected-value math on existential risk is famously slippery, but you don't need precise numbers. When the downside is total and irreversible, even a small annual probability compounds into something you should not ignore over civilizational timescales.] When the downside is total and irreversible, the rational response is not to compute the exact odds. It's to buy insurance. A self-sustaining settlement off Earth is the only insurance policy that actually covers the catastrophic case, because it's the only one that survives the loss of the thing being insured.

That's the floor of the argument, and it's enough on its own. But it isn't the part I find most compelling.

## The offensive argument

The part I find most compelling is what a frontier does to the people who have one.

Civilizations are partly held together by the size of the future they believe in. When that future is large and open — when there is somewhere genuinely new to go — a culture organizes its energy around building toward it. When the future feels closed, that same energy turns inward and sour, and gets spent fighting over the existing pie instead of baking a larger one. A frontier is not just real estate. It's a shared answer to the question *what are we for*, and a civilization without a good answer to that question does not stay healthy for long.

Leaving Earth is the clearest frontier available to us. It is hard enough to demand our best, open-ended enough to absorb generations of ambition, and concrete enough that you can actually build toward it on a Tuesday. That combination is rare. We should not waste it.

## The honest objections

The strongest objection is that this is a distraction from problems here, and it deserves a real answer rather than a dismissal. My answer is that the two are not in competition the way the framing assumes — the engineering that gets a civilization off-planet is the same engineering that makes Earth more resilient: closed-loop life support, abundant energy, materials science, the ability to keep humans alive in hostile environments. The frontier pays a dividend back home before anyone emigrates.

The second objection is that it's the province of a handful of billionaires, and therefore not really *ours*. That one I take seriously, because it's currently true, and it's a version of the :trail[recapture pattern]{slug=the-recapture-pattern} — a liberation that could concentrate into very few hands. The work, then, is to make sure leaving Earth becomes a wide human project rather than a narrow one. That's a choice we are making now, by what we build and who we let build it.

And it pairs naturally with the other bet on a bigger future: :trail[longevity]{slug=biology-is-becoming-programmable}. More room, and more time to use it. Both are the same wager — that the human story is in its early chapters, not its last ones, and that the most serious thing an optimist can do is act like it.
