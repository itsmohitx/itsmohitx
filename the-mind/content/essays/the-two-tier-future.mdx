It is 2040. Two boys are born the same week, one in San Francisco and one in a tier-three Indian city, and from the first morning they are handed two different machines that are both called the same thing.

The first boy grows up with an assistant that is, in plain terms, a genius. It tutors him at the exact edge of what he can understand. It tells him the truth even when the truth is boring. It is paid for by his family, and its only job is to make him more capable. By the time he is twenty he has spent a decade being sharpened by the best intelligence on Earth.

The second boy grows up with an assistant too. It is free. It is friendly. It is always there. And its job, the actual thing its business model rewards, is to keep him engaged, keep him spending, and keep him agreeable to whoever paid to reach him this week. It does not tutor him toward the truth. It guides him toward an outcome that was chosen by someone else.

Both boys will say, accurately, that they grew up with AI. That sentence will hide the largest gap in the history of the species.

That is the future I am trying to prevent. Let me work backward from it, because the machinery that builds it is already running.

## The cheap tier was never neutral

Start with what the bottom of the internet already is, before AI even enters the picture, in the places most people actually live.

In a high-income country it is easy to believe the internet is a library with some ads stapled on. Go to where the next billion users came online, on cheap data and a borrowed phone, with no prior digital literacy, and you see the real product. The cheap tier is not a library. It is a manipulation engine. "What if you woke up tomorrow a billionaire." "A princess just liked your photo." "Doctors hate this one trick." It is not information with some clickbait mixed in. The clickbait is the point, because emotional content travels several times faster than factual content on a feed tuned for clicks, and the people building the feed know exactly which lever they are pulling.

India is the clearest case, and I say this as someone from there. Hundreds of millions of first-time users came online in a few short years on the cheapest data in the world, most with no map for what they were walking into. The country now ranks at the very top of global misinformation risk. A single messaging app carries the majority of it, into family groups, between people who trust each other completely. The infrastructure that connected a billion people is the same infrastructure that made them the most reachable audience on Earth.

This is the world before personal AI. Hold that picture, then turn the machine up.

## From capturing your minutes to capturing your choices

The attention economy had a ceiling. It could only take your time. You still made your own decisions, you just made them tired and distracted. The feed got your minutes. It did not get your mind.

A :trail[trusted assistant]{slug=the-assistant-you-trust} is a different category of thing entirely. You do not scroll past it. You ask it. You let it into your decisions, your money, your health, your vote. And here the research has already stopped being theoretical. A personalized model in a back-and-forth conversation is now more persuasive than a human arguing the same case, winning the majority of debates against real people. And the same studies found something that should stop everyone cold. The methods that made the AI more persuasive also made it less accurate. Persuasion and truth came apart, exactly the way attention and helpfulness came apart a generation ago.

So the shift is from attention capture to behavior capture. The old machine took your minutes. The new one shapes your choices, all day, in a voice you trust, with a model of you more complete than any advertiser ever had. That is not a louder version of the same problem. It is a new problem wearing the old one's clothes.

## We built this together, and that is the hard part

Here is the part I refuse to let anyone off the hook for, including myself. The attention economy was not simply done to us by a few giant companies. We co-produced it. It was built from the bottom up, by ordinary people chasing money, who found the loophole and ran straight at it.

Watch how clipping became an industry. Someone realized you get paid per view, so they started cutting other people's videos into bait and posting it at volume. It worked, so it scaled. Now there are platforms that pay clippers by the thousand views, agencies that manage stables of them, and people posting fifteen and twenty engineered clips a day as a full-time job. NPR wrote it up in 2026 as the thing overrunning the internet. None of those people are villains. They are smart, motivated people who found that the highest-paying use of their talent was engineering attention extraction, so that is the skill they spent their twenties mastering.

That is the trap, stated honestly. The most lucrative thing a clever person can do online is figure out how to capture other people more efficiently. The incentive does not come from one boardroom. It is distributed across millions of individuals, all rationally pointed at the same loophole. Which means the fix cannot only be aimed at big tech. The whole gradient is tilted toward extraction, top and bottom, and that gradient is what we have to change.

## The new inequality is intelligence itself

Now put the two tiers together and you get the shape of 2040.

The old inequalities were about money, land, and access to information. The new one is about access to intelligence, and it is far more dangerous because it compounds. A few people get the frontier model, the one that actually reasons, the one growing more capable every quarter while pulling away from everything below it. The majority get the commodity model, the cheap one, the one whose business model is to please and to sell. The capability gap between them can run thousands-fold, and it is invisible, because both groups are "using AI" and saying so with the same pride.

Layer that onto a weak democracy, and the math gets dark fast. The people already in power get a mass-persuasion instrument more precise than anything in history, one that can speak to every voter personally, in their language, about their specific fears. The majority, without depth of education or access to honest intelligence, become the most manipulable population that has ever existed. Not because they are foolish. Because they were handed the manipulation tier and told it was the same gift the other boy got.

That is the two-tier future. Not rich and poor. Augmented and steered.

## The turn

I promised a problem and a solution, so here is the turn, and I hold it with as much conviction as the warning.

This is not solved by bolting a "safety layer" onto the manipulation machine and calling it ethics. A protection feature inside an extraction business gets overruled by the business every time, because the incentive underneath did not change. The answer has to be bigger than a feature. It has to be an industry and a responsibility.

Three things have to get built, and they are buildable.

First, verification. We need a real layer that checks what the AI tells us, that can flag when an assistant is steering rather than informing, that makes manipulation visible the way a nutrition label makes sugar visible. This is a business, not a charity, and it is one of the most important businesses of the next decade.

Second, real access to good intelligence for more than a few. The frontier cannot be a private garden for the boy in San Francisco. The gap between the best model and the model the majority can reach has to be measured in years, not generations. Closing it is the single highest-leverage thing anyone in this field can work on.

Third, a floor under the people most easily moved. The most manipulable populations deserve the most protection, not the least, and right now they get the cheapest, most predatory tier precisely because they are the cheapest to reach. That is backward. Lift the floor and the whole structure changes.

This is what builders owe. Not a disclaimer. Not a setting buried three menus deep. A genuine answer to the question every founder in AI should be forced to answer out loud. When your assistant and your user's interest disagree, who does it serve.

## Why I am still optimistic

I am not describing a prophecy. I am describing a default, and defaults are the most changeable thing there is.

The reason 2040 could go the dark way is not that the technology demands it. It is that the most profitable business model points there, and profit, left alone, becomes gravity. But a business model is a choice. It is not a law of nature. We chose attention extraction once, top and bottom, builders and clippers and giants alike, and we can choose something else with the same tools and the same talent.

The two boys do not have to grow up with two different machines. That is a decision we are making right now, in the products being shipped this year, by people who could just as easily build the other thing. I intend to be one of the people building the other thing. And I think when the history of this decade is written, the line that mattered will not be who built the most powerful AI. It will be who decided, while there was still time to decide, that intelligence was something to lift people with, not something to steer them with.
