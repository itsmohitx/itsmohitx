The most profitable business model of our era is attention extraction. Build a feed, learn what holds the human eye, sell that attention to advertisers. It is one of the great money machines ever invented, and we keep scoring it as technology winning.

It is a drag. A company can be enormous, beloved by the market, and a net negative on the civilizational ladder. The attention machine does not carry us toward a long-lived, multiplanetary, flourishing future. It carries us toward a more captured, more distracted, more manipulated version of the present, and it does this because the economics reward exactly that.

We miss it for one reason. We measure companies by market cap and revenue, and we treat those numbers as if they measure progress. They measure economic growth. Civilizational acceleration is a different quantity, and the two have drifted far enough apart that the gap is now the whole story. On the market-cap scoreboard the attention machine looks like a champion. Change the scoreboard to net acceleration and the ranking inverts.

If you measure the wrong thing, you reward the wrong thing. We have been doing it for twenty years, pointing our best engineers at the problem of holding a human eye one second longer, and calling the result a win.

Braking forfeits the future. The answer is to measure. To say out loud, in public, which companies actually move us up the ladder and which only move their own balance sheet.

The full argument is in What Accelerates a Civilization.
