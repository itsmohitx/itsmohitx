Here is what people get wrong about space, and about every hard frontier. They look at how expensive and difficult it is today, file it under later, and wait for it to look reasonable before they take it seriously.

They have the timing exactly backward.

Deep science compounds like training a body. You put in the months and see nothing. You feel slower and sorer, with no visible payoff. The muscle is being built underneath the whole time, invisibly, and then the results arrive all at once. We are in the early, ugly, no-visible-results phase of becoming a spacefaring species. That is precisely the phase where most people quit, and precisely the phase where staying matters most.

The cost curve already proves the work is happening. Reaching orbit cost tens of thousands of dollars per kilogram for decades. Reusable rockets cut that by more than ten times, and the next vehicle aims to cut it again. Each drop makes a hundred new things possible that were fiction the year before. The people waiting for it to look obvious will arrive after the curve has bent, after the muscle is visible to everyone, which is the moment the early advantage is already gone.

The unglamorous early months are the part that counts. The people who start now, before there is anything to show, are the ones building the strength the rest will only see later.

The full argument is in The Case for Leaving Earth.
