When you worry that AI assistants will quietly shape what a population believes, the reflex answer is to bolt a protection layer onto the side. A safety toggle. A disclaimer. A filter someone adds at the end, after the real product is built and shipped. That answer is too small, and it loses.

A protection bolted on after the fact is always weaker than the business model it is bolted onto. The model that gently moves behavior hits its revenue targets better than the model that does not, so the nudging compounds, because the number has to go up. A filter does not beat an incentive. The incentive runs all day. The filter runs once.

The real fix is to make verification an industry. An economic engine and a profession on the protector side of capture, as serious and well-funded as the side doing the capturing. Independent checks on what these systems tell us. Standards for it. Companies whose entire reason to exist is confirming that the helpful voice is telling you the truth, and people whose careers depend on getting that right.

That reframes it from charity to responsibility. Asking the manipulation layer to please behave gets you nowhere. You build a counterweight with its own revenue, its own talent, and its own stake in the outcome, so that being trustworthy is itself a market worth winning.

Verification is the protector's half of the future, and right now almost nobody is building it.
